Teaching & Mentoring Philosophy

The best thing about doing a Ph.D. in astronomy is how much of it is making things intelligible — to yourself, to collaborators, to referees, to students, to the public. I have come to see teaching and research as the same craft, and I try to bring the same discipline to both: ask a question that matters, be honest about what you actually know, and design the next step so the learner (or the calculation, or the simulation) can actually succeed at it.

Three commitments shape how I run a classroom, a research group, or a one-on-one mentorship.

1 · Inquiry before content.

I was trained in inquiry-based STEM teaching through the UCSC ISEE Professional Development Program (PDP) — one of the most rigorous and well-studied programs in the country for turning active scientists into intentional teachers. Through ISEE PDP I designed and delivered an inquiry activity on statistics at UCLA (2019). I have also completed Columbia Center for Teaching & Learning (CTL)’s advanced-track Teaching Development Program (TDP, 2018–2024), and Columbia CTL’s Innovative Course Design Seminar (2021). I was also selected to be a Lead Instructor at the Yale Young Global Scholars (YYGS) program.

The single biggest thing I took from all of this: the learner has to do the thinking. A lecture that hands students a finished result trains them to recognise that result; a well-designed inquiry trains them to build the next one themselves. When I teach concepts in physics, I spend as much time designing what students will do as what I will say.

2 · Inclusion is a design choice, not a disposition.

Mentorship is a structural commitment. I write down what I expect from a student, I write down what they can expect from me, and we revisit both on a set cadence. I look for students at the beginning of their trajectory — summer undergraduates, Masters students, early PhDs — because that is the moment when a generous, well-scaffolded first project changes an entire career.

I have now mentored nine students across four institutions (IISER Kolkata, Pune University / Heidelberg / MPA-Garching, Columbia, Stanford, San Diego State, Kyoto). Several have gone on to PhDs (UC San Diego, Stanford, MPA-Garching), and one — Kohki Uno — is now a postdoc at Columbia with a co-authored paper out of the collaboration. I also served as the Head TA of the Columbia Astronomy Department (2020–2021).

3 · The course outlives the course.

The deliverable of a class is not an exam but a habit of mind. I treat every course I teach — Intro to Astronomy labs and guest lectures at Columbia (Fall 2019, Spring 2020), guest lectures, outreach lectures at IISER Bhopal, TIFR Mumbai, Columbia’s outreach program (≈ 750 high-school students), and my mentorship of Democracy Prep High School students in computer science — as an opportunity for students to pick up tools they will still be using ten years later: how to formulate a question, how to read a paper, how to estimate an order of magnitude, how to fail gracefully at a calculation and try again.

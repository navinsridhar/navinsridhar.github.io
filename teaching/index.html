Teaching & Mentoring Philosophy

The best thing about doing a PhD in astrophysics is how much of it is making things intelligible — to yourself, to collaborators, to referees, to students, to the public. I have come to see teaching and research as complementary crafts, and I try to bring the same discipline to both: ask a question that matters, be honest about what you actually know, and design the next step so the learner (or the calculation, or the simulation) can actually succeed at it.

Three commitments shape how I run a classroom, a research group, or a one-on-one mentorship.

1 · Inquiry before content.

I was trained in inquiry-based STEM teaching through the UCSC ISEE Professional Development Program (PDP) — one of the most rigorous and well-studied programs in the country for turning active scientists into intentional teachers. Through ISEE PDP I designed and delivered an inquiry activity on measurement uncertainties at UCLA. I also completed Columbia Center for Teaching & Learning (CTL)’s Teaching Development Program (2018–2024) and CTL’s Innovative Course Design Seminar (Fall 2021), a five-week learner-centered seminar built around backward design, universal design for learning, and inclusive assessment — culminating in a peer-reviewed, context-specific course syllabus.

The single biggest thing I took from all of this is that the learner has to do the thinking, and my role is to enable it. A lecture that hands students a finished result trains them to recognise that result; a well-designed inquiry trains them to build the next one themselves. This is what I mean by student-driven learning: the student, not the instructor, owns the question, sets the pace, and decides which dead ends are worth chasing — while I provide the scaffolding, the prompts, and the honest feedback that keep the exploration productive. When I teach concepts in physics, I spend as much time designing what students will do as what I will say. Concretely: I begin each session with a short visible roadmap of the day’s goals; I open with a provocation rather than a definition; and, before students attempt a new calculation, we work a parallel example together so they enter the activity having seen the approach to solving a problem, not just the formula.

2 · Inclusion and accessibility is a design choice, not a disposition.

Inclusive teaching, to me, is not a tone — it is a set of structural choices that determine who can actually participate in a classroom or a research project. Following ICDS, I treat universal design for learning as the default rather than as accommodation: I provide the same content in multiple modalities (a visual diagram, a verbal walkthrough, a written handout), I distribute slides and notes ahead of time so students who process best by reading don’t have to keep up in real time. I make office hours and asynchronous Q&A for students who can’t comfortably ask in front of their peers.

In recruitment and mentorship, I deliberately look at the earliest stage of the trajectory — summer undergraduates, Masters students, first-year PhDs — and especially at students who haven’t already been routed onto an elite track, because that is the moment when a generous, well-scaffolded first project changes a career. With each new mentee I communicate what I expect from them and what they can expect from me — meeting cadence, authorship norms, response times, vacation policy, and what to do when they get stuck — and we revisit these expectations on a fixed schedule. Putting expectations out explicitly takes the hidden curriculum out of the mentorship relationship, which disproportionately benefits students who didn’t grow up already knowing the unwritten rules of academia.

Accessibility shows up in the small choices too: high-contrast slides with large fonts, captions on any video I share, descriptive alt-text on plots, microphones used even in small rooms, and a default of low-stakes formative check-ins (a one-page plot critique, a back-of-the-envelope estimate, a five-minute talk) so that students who learn by iterating — rather than performing — get repeated, non-punitive chances to course- correct before any high-stakes deliverable.

3 · Teach the transferable skill, not the curriculum item.

The deliverable of a class is not an exam, and the deliverable of a research project is not a paper — both are the habits of mind the student walks away with. Across very different settings (an undergraduate Astronomy labs, course lectures, outreach lectures, high-school mentoring at Harlem Democracy Prep High School, or one-on-one PhD-track research mentoring), I try to optimise for these handful of skills that compound over a career:

  • Formulating a question that can actually be answered. Most students arrive with diffuse curiosity (“how do black holes work?”); the work is to refine that into something falsifiable that fits a semester or a homework set. We iterate on those questions until they pass that test.
  • Order-of-magnitude estimation. Before any simulation, plot, or numerical answer, I ask myself and the student what to expect — to a factor of ten — and why. This is the single skill that most reliably catches errors, and it is portable to every quantitative profession a student might end up in, not just astrophysics.
  • Reading a technical paper without being intimidated. I advise students getting into research and reading research papers to first peruse the abstract and figures, predict the conclusion, then read the methods. Students leave knowing that a published paper is a draft you are allowed to argue with.
  • Communicating the result to multiple audiences. Every project I co-author with students ends with two artefacts: a technical write-up for specialists, and a presentation for non-subject experts. The act of writing the second one almost always sharpens the first.

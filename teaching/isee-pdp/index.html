ISEE PDP — UCLA Inquiry Activity (2019)

Report on my UCSC ISEE Professional Development Program (PDP) inquiry-based teaching activity, delivered at UCLA in September 2019.

This page reproduces the report I submitted as a participant in the UCSC ISEE Professional Development Program (PDP), documenting an inquiry-based teaching activity I co-designed and delivered at UCLA. The original biography page lived at isee.ucsc.edu (mirrored on the Wayback Machine).

Teaching activity summary

  • Activity: “To err is human — Measurement uncertainties and significant measurements”
  • Venue: UCLA Physics/Astronomy PREP
  • Date: September 25, 2019
  • Learners: 20 undergraduate students

Reflection on teaching and assessing the practices of science

The STEM practice we concentrated on was “Constructing explanations.” In physics and astronomy we construct explanations to expound on our observations and experiments all the time, so it is an authentic STEM practice. Being able to construct an explanation using data, and to use reasoning to connect the explanation to a broader claim, is a critical component of any experimental science. Requiring students to construct scientific explanations to support their claims — and to develop the argumentation that defends those claims with the data — has been shown to enhance students’ understanding of scientific content (Zohar et al., 2002). The process of constructing and arguing explanations is also not just content-specific: it is broadly applicable, and transferable to non-STEM fields (Kuhn et al., 1993; Yeh et al., 2001).

To encourage the critical thinking required to construct explanations, our activity allowed students to come up with their own questions about a few scenarios and datasets. The questions they raised were followed by a claim or hypothesis based on a first look at the data; the students then unraveled their claims with logical tools, which became part of their explanation for why each claim was scientifically either true or flawed.

Common student difficulties

Following McNeill et al. (2006), we anticipated several recurring difficulties students face when constructing explanations:

  • Identifying which data are useful, and what constitutes “sufficient evidence” to support a claim.
  • Confirmation bias — discarding data that don’t match prior expectations (Chinn et al., 1991; Koslowski, 1996).
  • Verbal discussions dominated by the claim itself, rather than the evidence supporting it (Jiminez-Aleixandre et al., 2000).
  • Discomfort with the mathematical or statistical calculations that underpin many astrophysical explanations.
  • Understanding statistical principles without yet having the intuition to apply them in physical contexts.

Designing for those difficulties

We addressed these difficulties through several deliberate design choices:

  • A pre-activity survey to gauge each student’s prior mathematical and statistical background, so we could plan our facilitation strategy.
  • An explicit, three-dimensional practice rubric (CER):
    • Claim — proposes an answer to the original question.
    • Evidence — relevant data used to support the claim.
    • Reasoning — logic that links the evidence to the claim.
  • A worked example: students had to justify whether their data contained a significant detection (e.g. of an atomic or molecular emission line) using confidence intervals. The data formed the evidence; the statistical analysis (weighted average, standard deviation, confidence level) formed the reasoning required to construct the explanation. Content goals and practice goals were designed to move hand-in-hand.

Culminating Assessment Task (CAT)

The CAT was designed to mimic the authentic STEM practice of individual paper writing, followed by an anonymized peer review and the dissemination of science to a general audience through a poster presentation:

  1. Students received the practice rubric (Claim / Evidence / Reasoning) before the individual paper-writing segment, along with explicit expectations for what qualifies as a nuanced, good, fair, or poor understanding.
  2. Each student wrote up their work, then participated in an anonymized peer review in which they used the same rubric on a peer’s paper.
  3. Students received their feedback before a final poster jigsaw presentation, which gave them an opportunity to improve.

The emphasis was on reviewing rather than grading, and on learning from other teams’ strengths and weaknesses. We adopted a “no one is being judged” policy to avoid a hostile learning environment.

## Teaching & Mentoring Philosophy

The best thing about doing a PhD in astrophysics is how much of it is _making
things intelligible_ — to yourself, to collaborators, to referees, to
students, to the public. I have come to see **teaching and research as complementary crafts**, and I try to bring the same discipline to both: ask a question
that matters, be honest about what you actually know, and design the next
step so the learner (or the calculation, or the simulation) can actually
succeed at it.

Three commitments shape how I run a classroom, a research group, or a
one-on-one mentorship.

### 1 · Inquiry before content.

I was trained in **inquiry-based STEM teaching** through the
[UCSC ISEE Professional Development Program (PDP)](https://isee.ucsc.edu/) —
one of the most rigorous and well-studied programs in the country for
turning active scientists into intentional teachers. Through ISEE PDP I
designed and delivered an [inquiry activity on measurement uncertainties
and significance at UCLA](/teaching/isee-pdp/) (Sept 2019). I
also completed Columbia Center for Teaching & Learning (CTL)'s advanced-track
**Teaching Development Program** (TDP, 2018–2024) and CTL's
**Innovative Course Design Seminar** (ICDS, Fall 2021), a five-week
learner-centered seminar built around backward design, universal design for
learning, and inclusive assessment — culminating in a peer-reviewed,
context-specific course syllabus. I was also selected as a Lead Instructor
at the Yale Young Global Scholars (YYGS) program.

The single biggest thing I took from all of this: _the learner has to do
the thinking._ A lecture that hands students a finished result trains them
to recognise that result; a well-designed inquiry trains them to build the
next one themselves. When I teach concepts in physics, I spend as much time
designing what students will _do_ as what I will _say_. Concretely: I begin
each session with a short visible roadmap of the day's goals; I open with a
provocation rather than a definition; and, before students attempt a new
calculation, we work a parallel example together so they enter the activity
having seen the form of an answer, not just the formula.

### 2 · Inclusion is a design choice, not a disposition.

Inclusive teaching, to me, is not a tone — it is a set of structural
choices that determine who can actually participate in a classroom or a
research project. Following ICDS, I treat **Universal Design for Learning
(UDL)** as the default rather than as accommodation: I provide the same
content in multiple modalities (a visual diagram, a verbal walkthrough, a
written handout), I distribute slides and notes ahead of time so students
who process best by reading don't have to keep up in real time, and I make
office hours and asynchronous Q&A first-class rather than fallback channels
for students who can't comfortably ask in front of their peers.

In recruitment and mentorship, I deliberately look at the **earliest stage**
of the trajectory — summer undergraduates, Masters students, first-year
PhDs — and especially at students who haven't already been routed onto an
elite track, because that is the moment when a generous, well-scaffolded
first project changes a career. With each new mentee I write down what I
expect from them and what they can expect from me — meeting cadence,
authorship norms, response times, vacation policy, and what to do when
they get stuck — and we revisit that document on a fixed schedule. Putting
expectations on paper takes the **hidden curriculum** out of the mentorship
relationship, which disproportionately benefits students who didn't grow up
already knowing the unwritten rules of academia.

Accessibility shows up in the small choices too: high-contrast slides with
large fonts, captions on any video I share, descriptive alt-text on plots,
microphones used even in small rooms, and a default of **low-stakes
formative check-ins** (a one-page plot critique, a back-of-the-envelope
estimate, a five-minute talk) so that students who learn by iterating —
rather than performing — get repeated, non-punitive chances to course-
correct before any high-stakes deliverable.

### 3 · Teach the transferable skill, not the curriculum item.

The deliverable of a class is not an exam, and the deliverable of a
research project is not a paper — both are the **habits of mind** the
student walks away with. Across very different settings (an undergraduate
Intro-to-Astronomy lab at Columbia, a guest lecture for ASTR UN1403, an
outreach lecture at IISER Bhopal or TIFR Mumbai, an astronomy event for
≈ 750 high-school students at Columbia, Computer-Science mentorship at
Democracy Prep High School, and one-on-one PhD-track research mentoring),
I try to optimise for the same handful of skills that compound over a
career:

- **Formulating a question that can actually be answered.** Most students
  arrive with diffuse curiosity ("how do black holes work?"); the work is
  to refine that into something falsifiable that fits a semester or a
  homework set. We rewrite questions out loud until they pass that test.
- **Order-of-magnitude estimation.** Before any simulation, plot, or
  numerical answer, I ask the student what they expect — to a factor of
  ten — and why. This is the single skill that most reliably catches
  errors, and it is portable to every quantitative profession a student
  might end up in, not just astrophysics.
- **Reading a paper without being intimidated by it.** I run paper-reading
  with mentees as a structured exercise: skim the abstract and figures,
  predict the conclusion, then read the methods. Students leave knowing
  that a published paper is a draft you are allowed to argue with.
- **Failing gracefully at a calculation.** When a result is wrong I model,
  out loud, the diagnostic moves I would make myself — bisect the
  pipeline, re-derive the limit, sanity-check units, talk to a colleague.
  The lesson is that being stuck is information, not embarrassment.
- **Communicating the result to two audiences.** Every project I co-author
  with a student ends with two artefacts: a technical write-up for
  specialists, and a one-paragraph plain-language summary for a friend in
  another field. The act of writing the second one almost always sharpens
  the first.

I can see this throughline across my mentorship outcomes — coauthored
papers with Kohki Uno (now a Columbia postdoc) and Sanya Gupta (now a
Stanford PhD student), transitions into PhD programmes at UC San Diego,
Stanford, and MPA-Garching, and Akash Vani's path from Pune to a Masters
at Heidelberg to a PhD at MPA — and equally in the formative outreach work
that doesn't show up in any paper at all. The Democracy Prep students did
not need to learn Python in order to become astrophysicists; they needed
to learn that they could break a problem into pieces and ask a stranger
for help, and that is the skill I was actually teaching.

### What this looks like in the classroom

The instincts above were sharpened — and pressure-tested — by formal
teaching observations during my PhD. A 2019 mid-course review of my
Astronomy Lab II section (Columbia ASTRW1904, conducted by Columbia CTL)
reported that students consistently described the climate as
**welcoming and low-pressure**, my enthusiasm for the material as
"contagious," and the lab as a place where they felt comfortable asking
questions both during and after class. Students particularly singled out
**diagrammatic/overview openings to each class**
that most made the rest of the session more accessible.

A peer observation of my Astronomy Lab I section in 2020
(Columbia UN1903, observed by a CTL graduate teaching consultant) found
that I was a _compelling speaker_ with _complete command over the topic_,"
that I varied lecture / circulation / blackboard work to keep the room
engaged, and — importantly — that I responded to students reponses, even wrong ones, with
"Don't worry, that's how experiments work" before walking the student to
the right one. Several practices I have adopted since — agenda slide on the
board at the start of class, a deliberate "stop and check the level"
pause before diving into mechanism, and circulating evenly to back rows as
well as front — come directly out of those observations.

This is the loop I want every classroom and mentorship I run to embody:
clear goals, low-stakes practice, honest feedback, and a structural bias
toward the student doing the thinking out loud.

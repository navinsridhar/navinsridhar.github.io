## Teaching & Mentoring Philosophy

The best thing about doing a Ph.D. in astronomy is how much of it is _making
things intelligible_ — to yourself, to collaborators, to referees, to
students, to the public. I have come to see **teaching and research as the
same craft**, and I try to bring the same discipline to both: ask a question
that matters, be honest about what you actually know, and design the next
step so the learner (or the calculation, or the simulation) can actually
succeed at it.

Three commitments shape how I run a classroom, a research group, or a
one-on-one mentorship.

### 1 · Inquiry before content.

I was trained in **inquiry-based STEM teaching** through the
[UCSC ISEE Professional Development Program (PDP)](https://isee.ucsc.edu/) —
one of the most rigorous and well-studied programs in the country for
turning active scientists into intentional teachers. Through ISEE PDP I
designed and delivered an inquiry activity on statistics at UCLA (2019). I
also completed Columbia Center for Teaching & Learning (CTL)'s advanced-track
**Teaching Development Program** (TDP, 2018–2024) and CTL's
**Innovative Course Design Seminar** (ICDS, Fall 2021), a five-week
learner-centered seminar built around backward design, universal design for
learning, and inclusive assessment — culminating in a peer-reviewed,
context-specific course syllabus. I was also selected as a Lead Instructor
at the Yale Young Global Scholars (YYGS) program.

The single biggest thing I took from all of this: _the learner has to do
the thinking._ A lecture that hands students a finished result trains them
to recognise that result; a well-designed inquiry trains them to build the
next one themselves. When I teach concepts in physics, I spend as much time
designing what students will _do_ as what I will _say_. Concretely: I begin
each session with a short visible roadmap of the day's goals; I open with a
provocation rather than a definition; and, before students attempt a new
calculation, we work a parallel example together so they enter the activity
having seen the form of an answer, not just the formula.

### 2 · Inclusion is a design choice, not a disposition.

Mentorship is a structural commitment. I write down what I expect from a
student, I write down what they can expect from me, and we revisit both on
a set cadence. I look for students at the beginning of their trajectory —
summer undergraduates, Masters students, early PhDs — because that is the
moment when a generous, well-scaffolded first project changes an entire
career. Following ICDS, I now build assessment scaffolding into mentorship
itself: short formative check-ins early on (a one-page plot critique, a
back-of-the-envelope estimate, a five-minute talk), so mentees and I can
correct course before any high-stakes deliverable.

I have now mentored **nine students** across four institutions (IISER
Kolkata, Pune University / Heidelberg / MPA-Garching, Columbia, Stanford,
San Diego State, Kyoto). Several have gone on to PhDs (UC San Diego,
Stanford, MPA-Garching), and one — Kohki Uno — is now a postdoc at Columbia
with a co-authored paper out of the collaboration. I also served as the
**Head TA of the Columbia Astronomy Department** (2020–2021).

### 3 · The course outlives the course.

The deliverable of a class is not an exam but a habit of mind. I treat
every course I teach — Intro to Astronomy labs and guest lectures at
Columbia (Fall 2019, Spring 2020), guest lectures, outreach lectures at
IISER Bhopal, TIFR Mumbai, Columbia's outreach program (≈ 750 high-school
students), and my mentorship of Democracy Prep High School students in
computer science — as an opportunity for students to pick up tools they
will still be using ten years later: how to formulate a question, how to
read a paper, how to estimate an order of magnitude, how to fail gracefully
at a calculation and try again.

### What this looks like in the classroom

The instincts above were sharpened — and pressure-tested — by formal
teaching observations during my PhD. A 2019 mid-course review of my
Astronomy Lab II section (Columbia ASTRW1904, conducted by Columbia CTL)
reported that students consistently described the climate as
**welcoming and low-pressure**, my enthusiasm for the material as
"contagious," and the lab as a place where they felt comfortable asking
questions both during and after class. Students particularly singled out
**diagrammatic openings to the lab** — drawing the configuration of the
celestial bodies on the board before the experiment — as the part of class
that most made the rest of the session usable.

A peer observation of my Astronomy Lab I section in 2020
(Columbia UN1903, observed by a CTL graduate teaching consultant) found
that I was a "compelling speaker" with "complete command over the topic,"
that I varied lecture / circulation / blackboard work to keep the room
engaged, and — importantly — that I responded to a wrong answer with
"Don't worry, that's how experiments work" before walking the student to
the right one. The same observation also identified a real failure mode:
when students asked an open-ended question, my answers occasionally
**over-elaborated into technical sidetracks** that confused rather than
clarified. Several practices I have adopted since — agenda slide on the
board at the start of class, a deliberate "stop and check the level"
pause before diving into mechanism, and circulating evenly to back rows as
well as front — come directly out of those observations.

This is the loop I want every classroom and mentorship I run to embody:
clear goals, low-stakes practice, honest feedback, and a structural bias
toward the student doing the thinking out loud.
